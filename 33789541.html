Author

Xuanning Zhang

Several unexpected challenges arose during this analysis. ABARES’s Excel files used hyphens (e.g., “2000–01”) in fiscal year labels instead of standard dashes, which caused the filter() function to return an empty data frame without issuing an error message. The solution was to use the slice() function and specify a fixed row position, thereby bypassing string matching entirely.

The second issue was inconsistent column types across the state tables, causing the pivot_longer() function to fail. This was resolved by converting all columns to string type before transposing the data. The Northern Territory’s aquaculture data included NA values for years with no aquaculture activity, so replace_na(aqua_vol, 0) was used to correctly retain these rows.

Main Question

How has the balance between wild-caught fisheries and aquaculture production in Australia changed over time?

Sub-problem

  1. Which states are most responsible for the growth in aquaculture production?

  2. Has Australia undergone a structural shift from capture fisheries toward aquaculture at the national level since 2000?

Reason

  • Significance: Australia is a major fishing nation, the research is related to food sustainability and marine environmental policy.

  • Personal: Interested to environmental policy and fishery.

Data description

The datasets use for this research is to analyze the structural shifts between wild-caught fisheries and aquaculture production in Australia. For more details, including licence information and definitions for the processed data used in analysis, refer to the README file and data-dictionary.

Dataset 1

Source

Australian Bureau of Agricultural and Resource Economics and Sciences (ABARES), Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry.

Content: Annual production statistics reporting volume and value in AUD for wild-caught fisheries and aquaculture in national level and by state. The time range is from 1998-99 to 2023-24.

Data Type: Census

Licence: Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International (CC BY 4.0)

Limitation: State-level table may apply slightly different species groupings through years which will make long-run comparisons less reliable.

Ethics & Privacy: No individual or personal identifiable information included in this dataset, and this dataset is publicly available with no privacy concerns.

#read the national data in table 3
#skip top 7 rows
raw_t3 <- read_xlsx(
  here("data", "raw", "AFAS_Data_2024-25_v1.0.0.xlsx"),
  sheet = "Table 3",
  skip  = 7
)

#tidy out the commodity of wild-caught with the unit of per kilo AUD.
wild_national_val <- raw_t3 |> 
  slice(35) |> 
  select(-1, -2, -3) |> #first three colunm are useless
  pivot_longer(everything(),  #transfer to long format, easy to 
               names_to = "year",  #read
               values_to = "wildcatch_val",
               values_transform = as.numeric) |> 
  filter(!is.na(wildcatch_val)) #drop the na value in 2024-25

#read the quantity of wild-caught in the unit of tone
wild_national_qty <- raw_t3 |>
  slice(70) |>
  select(-1, -2, -3) |>
  pivot_longer(everything(),
               names_to  = "year",
               values_to = "wildcatch_vol",
               values_transform = as.numeric) |> 
  filter(!is.na(wildcatch_vol))


#read the aquaculture data in table 4
raw_t4 <- read_xlsx(
  here("data", "raw", "AFAS_Data_2024-25_v1.0.0.xlsx"),
  sheet = "Table 4",
  skip  = 7
)
#same way to calculate the commodity of aquaculture fishery
aqua_national_val <- raw_t4 |>
  slice(24) |>
  select(-1, -2, -3) |>
  pivot_longer(everything(),
               names_to  = "year",
               values_to = "aqua_val") |>
  mutate(aqua_val = as.numeric(aqua_val)) |> filter(!is.na(aqua_val))
#calculate the quantity of aquacultre fishery
aqua_national_qty <- raw_t4 |>
  slice(48) |>
  select(-1, -2, -3) |>
  pivot_longer(everything(),
               names_to  = "year",
               values_to = "aqua_vol") |>
  mutate(aqua_vol = as.numeric(aqua_vol)) |> 
  filter(!is.na(aqua_vol))

#join all data together and save
national_clean <- wild_national_val |>
  left_join(aqua_national_val,  by = "year") |>
  left_join(wild_national_qty,  by = "year") |>
  left_join(aqua_national_qty,  by = "year") |>
  mutate(
    total_val           = wildcatch_val + aqua_val,
    aqua_share_val      = aqua_val / total_val,
    wildcatch_share_val = wildcatch_val / total_val,
    total_vol           = wildcatch_vol + aqua_vol,
    aqua_share_vol      = aqua_vol / total_vol,
    wildcatch_share_vol = wildcatch_vol/ total_vol
  )

#save the clean data
write_csv(national_clean, here("data", "processed", "national_production_clean.csv"))

Wild Catch or Fish Farm? How Australia’s Seafood Industry Is Changing

What Are Wild-Caught Fisheries and Aquaculture?

Before delving into the data, it would be helpful to understand what we are comparing. Wild-caught fisheries refer to fish and seafood harvested directly from the ocean, rivers and lakes. Australia has one of the world’s largest exclusive economic zoes which covering around 8.2 million square kilometers, and its whild-caught fisheries including a wide range of species from southern bluefin tuna in South Australian to rock lobsters off the coast of Western Australia (ABARES, 2025).

In contrast, aquaculture is the rearing of aquatic organisms in controlled environments like offshore net pens, inland ponds and onshore tanks. In Australia, Atlantic salmon farmed in the cold and deep waters of Tasmania dominates the sector in terms of production value, there also have alongside oysters, mussels and barramundi (ABARES, 2025).

Both industries are of significant economic importance. ABARES (2025) has pointed out that Australian fishing and aquaculture industries collectively produces over $ 3.8 billion output value during 2023-24 financial year which supporting fishing dependent communities across the country.

Importance of Analytics on Balance of Wild-caught and Aquaculture

The impact of how a country obtains its seafood extends far beyond the dinner table. Aquaculture could provide a more stable food supply and reduce pressure on wild fish population in theory (Naylor et al., 2000). However, large-scale salmon farming will bring environmental costs like nutrient pollution and biosecurity risks to wild populations (Buschmann et al., 2006). Also, the national shift towards aquaculture means communities which rely on wild-caught fish will gradually lose their economic.

Data Analysis

Figure 1 show the total value of Australian fisheries production from 1998 to 2023 in financial years, the data has been separated into wild-caught and aquaculture. Wild capture was overwhelmingly dominant in late 1990s. However, the contribution of aquaculture grew steadily in las two decades while wild capture maintain largely unchanged. The value of aquaculture had surpassed that of wild caught which confirmed by ABARES (2025).

Figure 2 has clearly point out the structural transformation. In 1998, there was only 28% of total fisheries value, wild-caught fisheries has dominated the industry by a wild margin. But in next 20 years, the aquaculture’s share climed steady and reached around 57% of total by 2023 and firstly broke 50% in 2020 (ABARES, 2025). This argument has been strengthened by Figure 3. Even the production is measured by volume rather than value, aquaculture’s share has confirmed that the transition is not the pricing effect driven by premium salmon prices, but reflect the structural growth in framed output. In summary, both value and volume has pointed the same direction: Australia’s seafood industry has shifted from wild-caught to aquaculture, and the trend shows no sign of reversing till now.

Figure 4 has transfer the focus form national trend to states. The map show the share of aquaculture of total fisheries value in 2023-24 in each states. Tasmania stands out as an outlier, its fisheries are mostly from its aquaculture which started from the rapid expansion of Atlantic salmon farming in 1990s (ABARES, 2025). South Australia and Victoria has a higher aquaculture share, but Western Australian and Queensland are still rely primarily on wild-caught fish. The Northern Territory records the lowest aquacultre share of all jurisdictions.
